The Choice

Two ways this plays out

The Klarna Path

  • Automate everything, move fast, cut headcount
  • Discover AI can't handle complexity at the volume you assumed
  • Quietly rehire; savings evaporate; brand damage doesn't
  • Result: you've made the same mistakes cheaper

The Ikea Path

  • AI absorbs commodity work: lookups, drafts, logging
  • Agents move up the value chain — genuine advisors, not process executors
  • Agent expertise gets focused on the calls that actually need it
  • Result: you scale capacity without scaling headcount

Enablement + Adoption

Why 85% aren't using it

  • 85% aren't using it because no one redesigned the work around it. The tool was available; it just didn't fit anywhere.
  • Mandating usage moves the weekly active metric. It doesn't move anything else. Agents comply; the work doesn't change.
  • Experienced agents built their value on policy expertise. An AI that retrieves policy doesn't feel neutral to them — it threatens something real. The design has to give their expertise a role, not sideline it.

The product growth lens

Think of the workforce as the user base and the AI workflow as the product. The adoption problem breaks down the same way:

  • Awareness: knowledge share, supervisor champions
  • Activation: policy question answered in 20 seconds, not 6 minutes — that's the aha moment
  • Retention: the tool saves real time every shift
  • Growth loop: early adopters become peer advocates; usage compounds without a mandate

Most internal rollouts stall at activation — the first use doesn't deliver enough value, and the tool is abandoned before the habit forms.

The Target

The pick: Agent Knowledge & Ramp

Why this first

  • Foundation: policy lookup is the prerequisite for disruption decision support, refund consistency, everything else. Build the layer once; every subsequent workflow benefits.
  • Always-on signal: disruption is 18 days/year. Knowledge gaps are every call, every day, 3,000 employees.
  • Safest pilot: no live customer decisions at risk during testing. Shadow mode is genuinely low-stakes.
  • Adoption flywheel: new agents learn AI as their default. As they become veterans, that behavior stays. Habit becomes the default without needing a mandate.
  • Fast proof: policy lookup speed is measurable in week 3. We don't wait 12 weeks for signal.
  • Unlocks the roadmap: disruption decision support and email drafting both build on this same knowledge layer — the foundation is shared.
  • What about disruption booking? Compelling use case, clear AI fit — but you want a proven system for your 18 highest-stakes days of the year, not an emerging one. We build to it, not with it.

How I'd confirm the pick

  • Shadow 6–8 agents: count how many times/call they search a policy doc or ask a supervisor
  • Pull supervisor escalation logs — what % are policy lookup questions?
  • Interview 5 new agents in their first month: what's the hardest part?

Design principles

  • Every answer cites its source. Agent can verify directly — and citations are what make the eval loop scorable. Trust and measurability from the same design decision.
  • Flags the edge case (WCHR pre-notification) that an agent might miss — this is where the system earns its value.
  • Plain language, not policy legalese. The answer is what the agent needs, not a policy quote.
  • No GDS access. Claude answers the question; the agent executes in Altéa. Clean separation of judgment and action.
Knowing It's Working

What we measure — and when

Leading indicators (visible weeks 4–6)

  • Weekly active users in pilot cohort
  • Policy queries per active user per shift
  • Supervisor escalation rate from new agents (target: down 30% by week 8)
  • Answer accuracy rate in eval loop — scored against known-good answers from Week 1 escalation logs
  • Email queue time (when wave 2 launches)

Lagging indicators (weeks 10–12+)

  • Ramp time: weeks to proficiency (target: 8–10 wks → 4–5 wks)
  • Handle time gap: new agent vs. veteran (target: 2× → 1.3×)
  • First-call resolution rate for new agents
  • CSAT scores: new-agent-handled calls

Enablement + Adoption

Scaling from pilot to division

  • Start with new hires. No ingrained habits to break. AI is the default from day one of training. Their success becomes social proof for veterans — "the people who just started are outperforming the curve."
  • Build conditions, don't mandate. Peer champions in each team. A visible win board showing before/after lookup times. Regular knowledge shares that feel approachable — not engineering-focused.
  • Design for the aha moment. Policy question answered in 20 seconds instead of 6 minutes. That's the moment behavior changes.
  • Shadow mode as trust-building. Supervisors see AI output alongside their own answers for 2 weeks. When they see it match — and sometimes catch something they'd have missed — that endorsement is worth more than any top-down mandate.

The flywheel

New agents adopt AI as default → faster to proficiency → become veterans who use AI natively → model the behavior for the next cohort → 35% annual attrition becomes the adoption engine, not the problem.

What doesn't work

  • Mandatory training sessions that feel like compliance
  • Top-down usage targets without workflow redesign
  • Rolling out to all 3,000 before proving it with 30

Appendix A1
Appendix — Common Objections

Handling pushback

"We already tried AI tools and nothing changed."

That's the most important context I could have. Tools don't change behavior — redesigned workflows do. The 6-month, 15% usage story tells me exactly what didn't work: Claude was available but no workflow was built around it. That's what this engagement fixes.

"Our agents are worried about their jobs."

A real concern — and it needs to be named at kickoff, not worked around. The goal is the Ikea model: AI absorbs lookup work, agents move up to judgment work. No airline running AI-assisted ops has cut frontline headcount — they've reallocated it to higher-value interactions.

"We can't share customer data with a third-party AI."

Claude Enterprise is designed for this. Customer PII stays within the airline's environment — it doesn't leave to train external models. The knowledge base contains policy docs, not customer records. Data governance is a design conversation in discovery, not a blocker.

"Why Claude and not the AI built into Salesforce / Microsoft / Genesys?"

Those native copilots are good — but each is locked to one vendor's data and roadmap. You'd get four disconnected assistants. Claude Enterprise is one reasoning layer that spans all of them. And the core problem — interpreting fare rules and policy consistently — is a reasoning problem, which is exactly where Claude leads.

"Why not just hire more agents?"

35% annual attrition means Cascadia is already on a treadmill — every hire takes 8–10 weeks to reach proficiency, and a third leave within a year. Hiring more doesn't solve knowledge transfer; it makes it worse at scale. The math only improves if ramp time drops and veteran-level performance arrives sooner.

"Won't training new agents first make veterans feel bypassed?"

Fair concern — and we address it directly at kickoff. Veterans run shadow mode in parallel: they see AI outputs alongside their own answers for two weeks. Their expertise is the benchmark the system is measured against. When they see it match — and sometimes catch something they'd have missed — that endorsement is worth more than any top-down push.

Appendix A2
Appendix — Risk Register

What could derail this — and how we design against it

Knowledge base returns a wrong answer

What it looks like: Agent cites an answer based on an outdated or ambiguous policy section on a live call.

Design against it: Eval test set from Day 1 — questions drawn from Week 1 escalation logs, scored against known-good answers. Shadow mode before any live use. Human judgment gate on every answer: the agent reads and decides. Nothing goes directly to the customer.

Veteran agents undermine the pilot

What it looks like: Experienced agents dismiss the tool publicly before it has a fair run — poisoning perception with the new hire cohort.

Design against it: Shadow mode makes their expertise the benchmark — they grade the system, not the other way around. The first two weeks give them authority over the evaluation. Their endorsement is earned, not assumed.

Policy changes outpace the knowledge base

What it looks like: A fare rule changes; the knowledge base answers with the old rule for weeks before anyone notices.

Design against it: Training team owns the refresh cadence from handoff. Policy update triggers a regression run against the eval test set. Version-controlled policy doc library with a change log.

A fourth risk worth naming: scope creep into integration work during Phase 1. The fix is an explicit scope boundary — Phase 1 runs in a browser tab, intentionally. Altéa and Salesforce integration is scoped for the extension.

Appendix — Extension Design

Disruption Rebooking: Scoped for Extension

Why it's Extension 1, not Wave 1

  • Only ~18 major disruption days/year — can't iterate quickly during pilot if events are sparse
  • Phase 1 requires GDS integration (PNR context + live inventory) — more complex build than knowledge base
  • But: we scope it completely in weeks 3–6 so it's ready to deploy the moment the next event hits

The decision support design

  • Agent pastes PNR context into Claude (or lightweight integration pulls it)
  • Claude has: reaccommodation policy, partner carrier inventory snapshot, passenger tier status, SSRs
  • Returns: top 3 rebooking options ranked by tier priority and connection risk, with relevant rule cited
  • Agent selects, executes in Altéa/Sabre — Claude doesn't touch the GDS

Appendix A6
Appendix — Workforce Evolution

What the Ikea path actually looks like for agents

As AI absorbs commodity work — policy lookups, draft responses, CRM logging — the agent's job changes. The question is whether that change is designed intentionally or left to chance.

Travel Consultant Advisors

For business accounts and top-tier loyalty members, experienced agents become proactive advisors — anticipating disruption before customers call, managing complex itineraries across carriers, and handling the moments where policy alone isn't enough. This is a job that's harder to automate and more satisfying to do.

Proactive Disruption Support

For loyalty members during an IROPS event: instead of waiting for the customer to call, AI identifies affected passengers, surfaces rebooking options, and agents reach out proactively. "We've already rebooked you on the 3pm — here are your options." This turns a service failure into a loyalty moment.

The role evolution arc

1

Today: Policy executor. Spends 40–60% of call time looking up rules, putting customers on hold, confirming with supervisors.

2

Wave 1–2: Augmented handler. AI retrieves policy instantly; agent focuses on judgment, tone, and de-escalation. Handle time drops. CSAT improves.

3

Extension: Trusted advisor. Handles complexity that AI can't — ambiguous situations, high-value relationships, proactive outreach. The job is harder to replace and more engaging to do.

The attrition implication

35% annual attrition is partly a job satisfaction problem. A role where AI handles the grind — and humans handle the judgment — is a meaningfully better job. Over 12–18 months, that shift may improve retention in ways that no hiring program can match.

Appendix A7
Appendix — Customer Experience

What the end customer actually experiences

Before — today's experience

Disruption scenario: Flight cancelled. Customer calls. Placed on hold for 45–60 minutes during peak demand. Agent takes another 6–8 minutes to find rebooking options. Final answer may differ from what a different agent would have given 20 minutes later.

Refund scenario: Submits a claim. Waits 35 days on average. No proactive status update. Receives a decision that 1 in 5 agents would have made differently. May need to escalate to get a second review.

Email scenario: Sends a complaint after a bad experience. Receives a response 4 days later. Tone is inconsistent with prior correspondence. No acknowledgment of their loyalty status.

After — redesigned experience

Disruption scenario: Agent gets rebooking options in 30 seconds, not 8 minutes. Hold time drops. For loyalty members, proactive outreach — "we saw your flight was cancelled and have already found you an alternative." Consistency improves because AI applies the same rules every time.

Refund scenario: AI applies the compensation matrix consistently. Agents spend less time on policy lookup, more time on edge cases that genuinely need judgment. Resolution time shortens as backlog clears. Fewer disputes because the first answer was right.

Email scenario: AI drafts a response within minutes; agent reviews and publishes. Queue goes from 4 days to same-day. Tone is consistent. Loyalty status is acknowledged. The customer feels seen, not processed.

The customer doesn't care whether AI is involved. They care that the answer was fast, correct, and consistent. That's what this engagement is designed to deliver.
